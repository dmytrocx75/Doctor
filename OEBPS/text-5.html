Dr. Osborne’s practice had blossomed from the start due to a lack of other general practitioners in the neighborhood. He had grown the business into a community institution with long-term patients whom he counted as friends. Despite the difficult schedule, he couldn’t imagine a better profession. He loved seeing his patients and enjoyed the charge he got
from solving their health challenges.

As George parked his car, he pondered the recent
changes that had become necessary to run a
successful medical practice. He now spent roughly
30 percent of his day on administrative tasks,
whereas when started his practice, it was closer to
10 percent. The persistent insurance reimbursement issues were

having a big effect on his bottom
line, and he was having trouble
keeping up. He had to let
his office manager
go for budgetary
reasons and now
Dr. Osborne
wasn’t just
treating patients.
At the end of each
day George would
meet with his staff

to review the next day’s schedule, return calls from patients and other doctors, and follow up on outstanding coverage concerns. Without an experienced scheduler, he had to review every appointment to ensure sufficient resources had been allocated and that all labs were properly noted. On top of those tasks remained several hours worth of researching patient conditions, updating charts, and writing referrals.

In addition to the turmoil within his practice, life outside his office was changing. Noise and traffic detours, due to construction of the new medical center, caused patient aggravation and cancellations.

Once or twice he even had to
have the construction workers’ cars towed out of his parking lot to make room for his patients and staff.
