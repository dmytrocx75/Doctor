Troubling News

George massaged his temples as he caught sight of the empty
desk, stack of mail, and ﬂashing red lights on the reception desk
phone. Just 7 in the morning, and a migraine was already starting.
At the top of the unopened mail pile on Mo’s desk, he noticed
a colorful ﬂyer announcing the grand opening date of the new
medical center.
“Only thirty days,” thought George, “and I am free from these
construction headaches!” He nervously neatened the papers on
the desk as he considered the other implications of this unwanted,
behemoth neighbor.
A big medical center facility in his neighborhood made him

really anxious. He’d been one of the few
sole practitioners in the area for almost
30 years. He’d been seeing many of
his patients for their whole lives. He
had shared in their joys and sorrows,
holding new babies and ushering
elderly patients through the aging
process, always conﬁdent that the
next generation would be coming to
his office. The new medical center
meant more doctors with different
care strategies and backgrounds.
His patients would certainly be
interested in the integrated services
and new equipment this healthcare
facility would be able to offer them.
He hoped his knowledge of patients’
health histories, their families, and
their personal lives would keep him
competitive, but he feared that
personal service was not enough to
keep him on top.
As George turned on the exam
room lights, he thought about the
new, digital world of healthcare.
